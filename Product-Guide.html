01 — Getting started

Getting started & sign-in

The console is a static web app — open it in any modern browser and sign in with the demo account. Nothing to install.

Open Ready Systems Console.html (or the deployed URL). The home page redirects you straight into the console.

The shell

A left sidebar lists the modules available to your role; the top bar shows who you're signed in as and lets you switch role or sign out. Everything centres on the work order number (the Siemens W/O No) — open any order to get its full workspace, or work across jobs from a module.

One account, many hats

For the demo, a single login can switch between Planning, Inventory, Quality and Director from the avatar menu — realistic for a small shop where one or two people wear several hats. In production these would be separate logins.

03 — Lifecycle

The order lifecycle & gates

One work order moves through eleven stages. Three gates (highlighted) stop work from running ahead of itself — you cannot buy before the client approves, and material cannot move without an inspection record.

01 RFQ02 Final BOM03 Costing04 Quote05 Approved ✓06 PO 07 Incoming QC ⛬08 Stores09 Build10 Final QC ⛬11 Dispatch
  • Lock the BOM → Costing. Costing can't be built on a moving target, so the harness is frozen first.
  • Send the quote → Quote, then record the client's approval → Approved. This gate opens procurement.
  • Raise / award a PO → PO. Goods inward logged in Stores → Incoming QC (receipt is automatic — no separate "mark received").
  • Accept material → Stores; issue to the job → Build.
  • Mark build complete → Final QC; pass final QC → Dispatch.

One job ID connects all four modules

Planning, Purchase, Stores and Quality all hang off the same W/O number. That's what makes the Director's single live view — and automatic, per-job procurement status — possible. Any order can be flagged stuck with a reason, which surfaces instantly on the dashboards.

04 — Workspace

The order workspace, step by step

Open any order to get a guided, gated pipeline for that one job. Each step unlocks the next; the tab strip shows where you are and what's locked.

  1. Documents

    Attach schematics, layout boards, GA drawings and datasheets. Optional, but they travel with the job.

  2. BOM

    The costing engine generates the harness from the config; you can add manual lines. Lock the BOM to freeze it and open costing. Revising a locked BOM bumps the revision and voids any approval.

  3. Costing & Quote

    See the full cost build-up, set the margin, and send the quote to the client. Price fields lock once approved.

  4. Approval

    Record the client's decision — approval (with their reference and date) or rejection. Approval is the gate that opens procurement.

  5. Procurement

    Order the material the job is short of — a direct PO, or float an RFQ to compare suppliers. Receipt updates automatically from Stores.

  6. Build & recovery

    Follow the job on the floor: material issued, build progress, final QC and dispatch. Mark the build complete to send it to QC; if QC rejects, the job returns here for rework.

An Overview tab summarises the whole job at a glance, and a "Next" banner always points to the single most useful action.

05 — Costing engine

The costing engine

The crown jewel: turn a Siemens configuration into a harness BOM and a price, without depending on one person's memory.

Type a config such as 8DJHST RRL+ME, motorised. The engine parses the feeder codes, assembles the harness from a feeder library (each feeder type maps to a set of wires, hardware and a build time), prices the material from the material master, and produces a cost and quote.

Cost build-up

MaterialLocked BOM × material-master rates
+ WastageA % of material for cutting offcuts & rejects
+ LabourTotal build-minutes × labour rate per minute
+ OverheadA % for power, floor & supervision
= Works costMaterial + wastage + labour + overhead
+ MarginThe one dial you tune per quote
= Quote totalShown in total and per-unit

These numbers are placeholders until validated

The feeder library, labour times and rates in this build are sensible defaults. They should be confirmed against real filled costing sheets and a handful of past orders with their quoted prices before the engine is trusted for live quoting.

06 — Quoting

Quoting & client approval Updated

Quoting is now a proper handshake: the quote goes out, sits in a waiting state, and is only marked approved when you record the client's actual decision — with evidence.

  1. Send the quote

    From Costing, send the priced quote. The order moves to Sent — awaiting client approval. Procurement stays locked.

  2. Record the client's decision

    When the client responds, open Record client decision:

    • Approved — capture the client's approval reference (their PO number or approval email) and the approval date. These are kept on the order as evidence.
    • Rejected — capture the reason. The quote returns to a revisable state so you can adjust and re-send.

  3. Procurement unlocks

    Only a recorded approval moves the order to Approved and opens procurement — you can never buy against a quote the client hasn't accepted.

Why the change

Previously the same person who sent a quote could instantly mark it approved, with nothing recorded. Now approval is deliberate and evidenced, and a rejection has a clear "revise & re-send" path.

07 — Procurement

The Procurement desk Rebuilt

A buyer's cockpit across every job, organised around the actual buying lifecycle — left to right, what to buy, source it, track the orders, then clear the bills.

Today

A single prioritised action queue: job shortfalls, bids to award, overdue POs, stock to replenish, and invoice exceptions — each with a one-click action.

To buy

All demand in one place — job shortfalls and stock replenishment. Raise a PO through one shared form, or float an RFQ.

Sourcing

Float RFQs, compare bids side by side with supplier reliability, and award — awarding raises the PO automatically.

Orders

Every PO: expedite open ones (ETA vs the job's need-by date), confirm supplier acknowledgement, and the full register.

Bills

Supplier invoices with a three-way match (PO price vs invoice vs GRN-accepted qty) and the MSME payment clock.

Suppliers

A scorecard blending on-time delivery (60%) and quality (40%) from the Quality module's own records.

Direct PO vs RFQ — and one buy path

For each shortfall the desk recommends the cheaper-to-act path: a direct PO to the preferred supplier for low-value or sole-source items, or get quotes when the spend is above the quote threshold and the material is multi-source. There is now one shared "Raise PO" form for everything — stock top-up or job shortfall — so there's a single, consistent way to cut a PO.

You can't double-order

Once quotes are out for a job, the direct-PO button is suppressed — award a bid to raise the PO instead. This removes the old trap where both "get quotes" and "raise PO" were live at the same time.

Receipt is automatic

A PO becomes Partial then Received as Inventory logs goods inward against it in Stores. There is no manual "mark received" step.

08 — Stores

Stores — inward, stock & issue

Inventory's three jobs: receive & inspect goods, hold running stock, and issue material to the job that needs it.

Inward + QC

Log goods inward against a PO (GRN, lot, challan, qty). Each lot gets an incoming-QC disposition — Accept, Rework or Reject — which writes an audit-ready record and updates the supplier scorecard. Logging inward moves the job to Incoming QC; acceptance moves it to Stores.

Stock on hand

Live running stock by item, with coil counts where relevant. This is what the procurement desk nets demand against, so you only buy the shortfall.

Issue to job

Issue material from stock to a work order. Issuing moves the job to Build — it's on the floor.

09 — Quality

Quality — final QC, recovery & KPIs Updated

The pre-dispatch gate, a rework loop for failures, an audit archive, and a KPI dashboard fitted to a contract panel & harness shop.

The build-complete gate

A job no longer appears at the QC gate the moment material is issued. The floor explicitly marks the build complete from the job's Build & recovery tab — only then does it enter the Final QC queue. Jobs still being built (including ones in rework) stay off the gate.

Final QC dispositions

DispositionWhat happens
PassJob moves to Dispatch, dated and ready to ship.
HoldStays at the gate for an in-place fix and re-inspection, with the reason recorded.
Reject → reworkGoes back to Build as recovery. It must be reworked, marked build-complete again, and re-inspected. The failed report stays visible on the job.

The recovery loop

A rejected job shows a recovery banner and its failed inspection report on the Build & recovery tab, so the floor knows exactly what to fix. Every Pass / Hold / Reject writes a Final-QC record into the audit archive.

Quality KPIs

Seven metrics chosen to fit a shop that builds a few dozen units for a handful of industrial clients — computed live from QC, inward and dispatch records:

  • First-pass yield — assemblies that passed Final QC clean the first time.
  • On-time delivery — dispatched on or before the committed date.
  • Supplier rejection — rework + reject share of inspected incoming lots.
  • COPQ (proxy) — value of rejected / reworked material against booked revenue.
  • Open complaints and average time to resolve.
  • Audit observations open and average closure time.

A supplier-quality table breaks incoming QC down by supplier (lots, accepted, rework, rejected, rejection %). PPM and NPS are deliberately not tracked — they mislead at this volume; rejection-by-lot and complaint-closure speed are the honest equivalents.

10 — Director

The Director view

A read-only live picture of the whole shop — no editing, just oversight.

The Director sees KPIs, the order board (every job and its stage), projects, the release board, purchase and costing, inventory, the Quality KPIs and quality records, plus an activity feed and stuck-job alerts. Because everything hangs off one job ID, this view is always current — there's no separate report to compile.

12 — Under the hood

How it's built & where the data lives

A deliberately simple, zero-infrastructure prototype — so it runs anywhere with nothing to set up.

Architecture

  • Static site — no server, no build step.
  • React rendered in the browser via an in-browser compiler.
  • The console loads a set of self-contained modules (store/costing engine, order workspace, planning, costing, purchase, stores, quality & KPIs).
  • Tailwind for styling, with the product's calm paper/ink palette.

Where data lives

All state lives in the browser's local storage, seeded with realistic demo orders, stock, POs, invoices and QC records. That means:

  • It runs offline and needs no accounts or database.
  • Data is per-browser — clearing storage or "Reset demo data" returns to the clean seed.
  • A production build would swap local storage for a shared backend; the module boundaries already anticipate that.

The costing engine is the piece to validate

The feeder library, parseConfig / generateHarness / computeCosting logic and the material rates are where the real value sits — and where a short walkthrough with the owner turns sensible defaults into trusted numbers.
